responce to the Booking.com survey

Today i answered to the Booking.com survey. I'm pretty sure that most of the feedback from the hoteliers will be screened out, so i'm posting my proposal for improvement here.

The question was: if you were booking.com CEO, what would you do to improve the services?

First of all i would take into account that hoteliers are not dumb uneducated entrepreneurs that only know how to manage their properties and serve tourists. Some hoteliers are highly educated engineers, IT and User Experience professionals that try to make their business as much automated as possible. However, Booking.com prevents them from doing that when it comes to reservation management.

For example in the contact form of the Extranet there is no such subject as "Technical issues" (do you really believe that your reservation system and the Extranet is perfect and there should be no technical issues?).

M2M-support is almost nonexistent. Extranet's front-end is changed few times a year in such a way that i have to partially rewrite our software which is used to handle the reservations. Changes are not announced. If an unfavourable change was made during my holiday, then the reservations must be manually handled until i fix the software.

Your developers do not respond to the technical queries, bug reports, etc. Instead, the communication between hoteliers and your software developers is screened by the persons working in Booking.com customer service mostly having poor technical background.

Yes, i know about channel managers, but we can't afford to use third-party services due to privacy and security issues. Therefore, direct M2M interface is a very basic thing that in my opinion must be available to all hotels.

avoid telemarketer fraud alike Q Marketing Solutions Oy

I have been living in Finland for 10 years and owned a company right from the beginning. I don't remember a single month without getting a phone call from a telemarketer. I can easily deal with them without getting fooled, but most foreigners especially the beginning entrepreneurs can be easily fooled by the telemarketers. One of the telemarketers is Q Marketing Solutions Oy. They started their business in march 2015. Not long ago their telemarketing methods were relatively acceptable: they mostly contacted small entrepreneurs having a business related to cleaning and building and offered them a place in catalogs like "siivous" and "kattoremontti" for about 30 to 35 EUR.

However, in the beginning of the summer their strategy was changed to a more aggressive one. Apparently they started to make unsolicited calls to selectively chosen small family-owned companies having more than a half of foreign names in the board of directors ("hallitus" in Finnish). First they start a friendly sounding discussion about the business in general and they mention Google corporate services ("Google Yrityspalvelut") making the victim believe that Google is currently updating the directory of companies and checking the information with the customer. They speak very fast and quite dirty (colloquial) Finnish when it comes to the fee, so the customer may not even notice it. They ask questions leading to your affirmative answers.

If you or your friend happens to be a victim of that company (i mean if you believe that it was not your intention to order the "google corporate services" from a completely unrelated third-party company), then simply don't pay attention to their warning about the collecting service Gothia Perintätoimisto. It's up to you to decide whether they made you make a binding contract in a honest way or dishonest way. You simply deny the existence of the agreement if you think you did not make it. There has been similar cases in Finland in 2011-2013 and the Helsinki district court (Helsingin käräjäoikeus) decided that it's not possible to prove the existence of a binding agreement if only phone records have been provided by the telemarketer as an evidence and the victim of the telemarketer denies the existence of the agreement. More information can be found here (in Finnish): https://www.yrittajat.fi/File/ac04a071-b2a8-49df-a582-bab19c2dd8fa/Helsingin_K%C3%A4O30062011_5.pdf

Even if you receive a letter from Varsinais-Suomen käräjäoikeus about "velka-asia", then the first letter is just a notice requiring you either to acknowledge the existence of the debt or declare it as groundless. It does not have anything to do with the trial itself yet.

If you have a positive or a negative experience about Q Marketing Solutions Oy, then you can leave your feedback here: https://www.facebook.com/qmarketingsolutions?sk=reviews

We must make the telemarketers stop exercising unethical practices in Finnish business culture!

Saturday, December 27, 2014

CryptoJS is a handy JavaScript framework comprising a set of cryptographic primitives that can be used together with the curve255js framework to implement a shared key agreement procedure. Unfortunately, both frameworks are documented quite poorly and I had to spend few hours before I could figure out why the key agreement between the client-side javascript code and the server-side PHP code did not work.

curve255js stores the parameters in its own internal format and the only usable way to serialize the generated ephemeral public key is to use the c255lhexencode() function provided by the framework. If your PHP code uses the curve25519_shared() function, then you need to know that the byte order is different. A workaround is to use the strrev() function:

$shared = strrev(curve25519_shared($my_secret, strrev($their_public)));

Now both the client and the server have the same shared secret. Another problem is that the CryptoJS.AES.decrypt() function was not designed to handle non-strings in an obvious way. The function will just return an empty object if you attempt to use it according to the documentation.

If your javascript code previously used the CryptoJS.AES.encrypt() function, then you can reuse the same CipherParam instance when using the CryptoJS.AES.decrypt(). You just need to modify it in the following way:

cipher_params.key = CryptoJS.enc.Hex.parse(key_hex);
cipher_params.iv = CryptoJS.enc.Hex.parse(iv_hex);
cipher_params.ciphertext = CryptoJS.enc.Hex.parse(ciphertext_hex);
var decrypted = CryptoJS.AES.decrypt(cipher_params, cipher_params.key, { iv: cipher_params.iv, mode: CryptoJS.mode.CBC, padding: CryptoJS.pad.ZeroPadding });

Quite surprising, isn't it ?
